The history of KFC, short for Kentucky Fried Chicken, began with Colonel Harland Sanders, an American entrepreneur born in 1890 in Henryville, Indiana, USA. Sanders worked in several professions throughout his early life, including as a farmhand, streetcar conductor, insurance salesman, and service station operator. During the 1930s, he started serving meals to travelers at his service station in Corbin, Kentucky. His fried chicken recipe quickly became popular among customers due to its unique flavor and cooking method.

In 1939, Sanders developed his famous recipe using a blend of eleven herbs and spices along with a pressure frying technique that allowed chicken to cook faster while remaining juicy and crispy. This secret recipe became the foundation of the KFC brand and remains one of the most closely guarded trade secrets in the food industry.

By 1952, Sanders began franchising his fried chicken business and opened the first official Kentucky Fried Chicken franchise in Salt Lake City, Utah, in partnership with Pete Harman. The name "Kentucky Fried Chicken" was chosen to emphasize the restaurant's connection to the southern state of Kentucky, which was known for its hospitality and cooking traditions.

The business expanded rapidly during the 1950s and 1960s as more franchise locations opened across the United States and internationally. In 1964, Sanders sold the company to a group of investors for approximately 2 million US dollars but continued to serve as the company's brand ambassador and public face for many years. His distinctive white suit, black string tie, and friendly personality became iconic symbols of the brand.

Throughout the following decades, KFC continued its global expansion and introduced new menu items while maintaining its signature fried chicken products. The company entered markets across Europe, Asia, Africa, and the Middle East, becoming one of the world's largest fast food chains. In 1991, the company officially adopted the shorter name "KFC" as part of a modernization effort, although Kentucky Fried Chicken remained its full name.

Today, KFC operates thousands of restaurants in more than 150 countries and territories worldwide. The brand is recognized for its Original Recipe chicken, famous secret seasoning blend, and the lasting legacy of Colonel Harland Sanders, whose image continues to appear in the company's logo and marketing materials.
