The History of Stripe


Founders and History
Founders
Stripe, Inc. was founded in 2010 by Irish brothers Patrick and John Collison. Patrick Collison, born on September 9, 1988, and John Collison, born on August 6, 1990, are both recognized for their significant contributions to the tech industry. Patrick attended the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), while John attended Harvard University. The idea for Stripe emerged from their frustration with the complexities of online payment systems, leading them to create a financial services company that simplifies online transactions. Their educational backgrounds at prestigious institutions like MIT and Harvard provided them with the technical expertise and entrepreneurial mindset necessary to tackle the challenges in the fintech industry.

The Collison brothers previously founded Auctomatic, an auction and marketplace management system, which they sold to Live Current Media in 2008 for $5 million. This experience gave them valuable insights into the e-commerce space and the technical challenges associated with online transactions. When developing Stripe, they encountered several technical challenges, including scalability, security, compliance, integration, and reliability. Their education at MIT and Harvard equipped them with the skills to address these issues effectively. For instance, Patrick’s background in computer science helped in designing a robust and scalable architecture, while John’s understanding of economics and business strategy contributed to navigating regulatory and market challenges.

Early investors, such as Peter Thiel and Y Combinator, played a crucial role in shaping Stripe’s business model and growth strategy. Their support provided not only financial backing but also mentorship and access to a network of industry experts. This guidance was instrumental in helping the Collison brothers refine their product and expand their market reach rapidly.

The Collison brothers’ experiences at MIT and Harvard were pivotal in their ability to navigate the regulatory and market challenges in the fintech industry. Their technical and strategic expertise, combined with early investor support, enabled Stripe to achieve rapid market expansion and establish itself as a leading player in the financial technology sector

History
2010: Stripe is launched, providing a simple way for developers to accept payments online. The company faced initial challenges such as competition from established payment processors like PayPal and Authorize.Net, regulatory hurdles, and the need to build trust with merchants and consumers. Stripe overcame these challenges by offering a developer-friendly platform, ease of integration, and a focus on providing a seamless payment experience, which helped it rapidly gain traction.
2011: Stripe raises $2 million in seed funding from investors including Peter Thiel and Y Combinator. This funding was pivotal for its growth, allowing the company to expand its team, enhance product offerings, and scale operations. Specifically, the funding enabled Stripe to invest in product development, improve its API, and establish a strong market positioning, thereby establishing itself as a major player in the online payments industry.
2012: The company expands its services internationally, launching in Canada and the UK. This rapid expansion was driven by the increasing demand for streamlined payment solutions in global markets and was enabled by Stripe’s robust infrastructure and strategic partnerships. Key factors in this successful expansion included localizing its services to meet regional regulatory requirements and forming alliances with local financial institutions.
2013: Stripe introduces its first product, Stripe Connect, allowing platforms to manage payments for multiple users. This innovation significantly boosted the company’s growth by enabling businesses to efficiently handle complex payment processes, thereby improving user experiences and facilitating seamless transactions. The introduction of Stripe Connect led to a tenfold increase in active users and expanded its reach to over 100 countries.
2014: Stripe raises $70 million in Series C funding, valuing the company at $1.75 billion. The primary objectives were international expansion and enhancement of product offerings. This funding led to significant growth in its customer base and the launch of new products and services. Strategic decisions behind this funding included focusing on expanding Stripe’s global reach and enhancing its payment processing platform to cater to a broader range of business needs.
2015: Stripe launches Atlas, a toolkit for starting an online business, and expands into additional countries. Atlas provided entrepreneurs with the resources needed to establish and grow their businesses online.
2016: The company raises $150 million in Series D funding, increasing its valuation to $9 billion. The objectives were to further global expansion and invest in new technology and infrastructure. The outcomes included continued international growth, the development of new tools for businesses, and an increase in market valuation.
2018: Stripe introduces new products like Stripe Terminal for in-person payments and expands its global reach. These new offerings diversified Stripe’s product portfolio and catered to a broader range of business needs.
2020: Stripe raises $600 million in funding, reaching a valuation of $36 billion. The funding supported the company’s continued innovation and expansion efforts.
2021: Stripe partners with major companies like Shopify and expands its offerings in Europe and Asia. These strategic partnerships and regional expansions helped Stripe solidify its presence in key markets.
2022: Stripe continues to innovate with new features and services, focusing on enhancing user experience and expanding its market presence. Notable introductions include Stripe Apps and the Stripe App Marketplace, which allow businesses to integrate third-party tools directly into their Stripe dashboard. Additionally, Stripe Financial Connections enables businesses to connect directly to customers’ bank accounts for streamlined payments and financial data access. Other key features launched were Stripe Tax for automatic tax calculations, Stripe Identity for customer verification, Stripe Climate for contributing to carbon removal projects, and the global expansion of Stripe Terminal for in-person payments. These enhancements significantly improved the platform’s functionality and integration capabilities, supporting business growth and operational efficiency.
2023: Stripe faced market adjustments, reducing its internal valuation to $50 billion from its peak of $95 billion. This adjustment was primarily due to broader economic conditions and market volatility. In response, the company focused on operational efficiency and launched new products, including Financial Connections and Data Pipeline. Financial Connections significantly improved payment success rates, reduced fraud, and provided better financial insights by enabling direct connections to customers’ bank accounts. Data Pipeline facilitated seamless data integration for businesses. Stripe also completed a $6.5 billion funding round at the reduced valuation, ensuring continued investment in innovation and growth.
2024: Stripe continues to strengthen its position with enhanced AI capabilities, expanded Treasury offerings, and improved developer tools. The company implemented advanced machine learning algorithms to predict and prevent fraudulent transactions more accurately, developed AI-driven tools for better financial insights and analytics, and streamlined user interactions for more personalized services. Stripe Treasury expanded to include a suite of financial services, allowing platforms to embed banking services such as bank account creation, fund transfers, and interest-earning accounts. These advancements have bolstered Stripe’s market position and operational efficiency, maintaining its focus on global expansion and technological innovation while adapting to evolving market conditions.

Investors
Stripe has attracted significant investment from notable figures in the tech industry. Early investors include Elon Musk and Peter Thiel, who were involved in the seed funding round. Musk and Thiel provided not only financial support but also strategic guidance, leveraging their extensive networks and industry experience to help Stripe navigate its early challenges. Thiel emphasized targeting a large market and solving significant problems, while Musk focused on product quality and user experience. Their guidance helped Stripe prioritize scalability and user-centric design, which were crucial in its initial development phase. In May 2011, Stripe received a $2 million investment from a group of venture capitalists, including Musk, Thiel, Sequoia Capital, SV Angel, and Andreessen Horowitz. This initial funding was pivotal, enabling Stripe to expand its team, refine its payment processing technology, and scale its operations, which positioned the company as a formidable player in the payment processing industry.

Subsequent funding rounds saw participation from major venture capital firms such as Sequoia Capital, Andreessen Horowitz, and Tiger Global Management. For instance, in January 2019, Stripe raised $100 million from Tiger Global Management, which significantly boosted its valuation to $22.5 billion. This increase was driven by rapid growth in its core payments processing business, international expansion, and the introduction of new products and services like Stripe Terminal and Stripe Issuing. Another notable round was in March 2023, when Stripe secured $6.5 billion in Series I funding, led by Andreessen Horowitz, valuing the company at $50 billion. This funding round was intended to provide liquidity to current and former employees, address employee tax obligations related to equity awards, and strengthen Stripe’s balance sheet. These investments have been instrumental in Stripe’s substantial valuation growth and market credibility.

Sequoia Capital has been a consistent supporter, even offering to buy up to $861 million worth of shares from its older funds to provide liquidity to investors. This move came as Stripe’s valuation reportedly reached $70 billion. The involvement of these high-profile investors has not only fueled Stripe’s financial growth but also enhanced its market credibility, making it one of the most valuable fintech companies globally.

Business Model
Stripe operates on a pay-as-you-go pricing model, meaning businesses only pay for the transactions they process without any setup or monthly fees. The standard transaction fee for online payments is 2.9% plus $0.30 per successful transaction for domestic cards. For international cards, the fee is typically higher, around 3.9% plus $0.30 per transaction. For in-person payments, the rate is 2.7% plus $0.05 per successful card charge. Stripe also offers customized pricing for businesses with large payment volumes or unique business models, which requires direct consultation with Stripe’s sales team. Criteria for customized pricing include factors such as payment volume, average transaction size, and the types of products or services being sold. Generally, businesses processing over $80,000 per month or having an average transaction size above $500 may qualify for customized pricing.

Additional services, such as Stripe Connect for platform management, have specific fees. Stripe Connect charges a platform fee of 0.25% of the payout volume, with a minimum fee of $0.25 and a maximum fee of $2.00 per payout. There is also a $2.00 fee for instant payouts. Other services like Stripe Billing, Stripe Radar, and Stripe Terminal have their own fee structures, which can impact overall costs for businesses. For instance, Stripe Billing charges 0.4% per paid invoice and 0.5% per recurring transaction, with advanced features costing 0.8% per transaction. Stripe Radar, a fraud prevention tool, has a fee of 5 cents per screened transaction, while Stripe Terminal charges 2.7% plus $0.05 per in-person transaction.

Stripe’s business model is designed to be flexible and scalable, catering to a wide range of businesses from small startups to large enterprises. For example, a small e-commerce store can easily integrate Stripe’s payment processing without incurring significant upfront costs, while a large corporation can negotiate tailored pricing based on their transaction volume and specific needs. This adaptability has made Stripe a popular choice among various types of businesses.

The scalability of Stripe’s business model is largely due to its cloud-based infrastructure, which allows it to handle a large volume of transactions efficiently. Stripe’s API-driven approach enables easy integration with various platforms and services, facilitating rapid adoption and expansion. Additionally, Stripe continuously innovates and expands its product offerings, such as billing, invoicing, and fraud prevention tools, to meet the evolving needs of its customers. This adaptability and comprehensive service portfolio contribute to its scalability and ability to support global operations.

Over time, Stripe’s business model has evolved from primarily offering payment processing services to a comprehensive suite of financial tools and services. Initially, Stripe focused on simplifying online payments for businesses by providing easy-to-integrate APIs. Over time, it expanded its offerings to include:

Stripe Connect: A platform for marketplaces and platforms to manage payments and payouts.
Stripe Atlas: A service to help startups incorporate in the U.S., which has facilitated the growth of over 20,000 startups from more than 140 countries, collectively raising over $3 billion in funding.
Stripe Issuing: Tools for creating, distributing, and managing physical and virtual cards.
Stripe Terminal: Solutions for in-person payments.
Stripe Capital: Financing options for businesses.
Stripe Treasury: Banking-as-a-service APIs for integrating financial services.
Stripe Radar: Fraud prevention tools.
Stripe Billing: Subscription management and invoicing.
Stripe Climate: A program to contribute to carbon removal projects.
Stripe has also expanded globally, supporting businesses in multiple countries and currencies. It operates in over 135 currencies and various payment methods, ensuring compliance with local regulations for secure and efficient payment processing. This global reach, combined with its robust infrastructure, enables Stripe to manage currency and regulatory differences across countries effectively
